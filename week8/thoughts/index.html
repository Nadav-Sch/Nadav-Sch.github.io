Thoughts

Nadav

What stood out to me was how BlackLash takes a simple arcade-style game and turns it into a message.

At first it just felt like a normal shooter, then I realized what the characters and enemies actually represented.

That moment made me see how Mongrel used gameplay to talk about racism and stereotypes in gaming.

It was a little uncomfortable but in a good way. It makes you think instead of just play.

I liked how it shows that even small design choices can reflect bigger social issues.

Overall, it changed how I look at games. Not just as fun, but as something that can make a statement or challenge people.

Sierra

As someone who loves games and values representation, I’m always paying attention to how non-white characters show up or don’t in gaming spaces, so BLACKLASH hit me on a deeper level.

At first, it stood out to me because it looked fun and interactive, and I didn’t immediately grasp its message beyond the surface.

But once I read about the context, the stereotypes it pushes back against, and Mongrel's intention, it felt affirming to see a game from 1998 confronting issues that are still so present today.

There was something powerful about seeing Blackness not as a side character, a joke, or a target, but as the force fighting back.

It made me appreciate BLACKLASH not just as a game, but as an early act of resistance, one that made space for players like me long before the industry cared to.

Ryan

BlackLash felt stripped down but sharp. You immediately understand who the enemies are and why you’re fighting them.

I liked that the message came through the gameplay instead of a cutscene.

It made me want to see more games put their politics right on the surface.

Who is Mongrel

Mongrel formed in London in 1995 when artists Mervin Jarman, Richard Pierre Davis, Graham Harwood, and their collaborators left community tech centers to build their own studio. They described themselves as a mix of artists, coders, and agitators who wanted to expose how race and class shaped the early web.

They ran street workshops with East London youth, built their own software tools, and hacked consumer hardware so the people being portrayed in media could speak for themselves. Their projects blended code, community archives, and loud imagery to show who was being left out of the so called digital revolution.

BlackLash, released in 1998, came from that mission. The team modified an arcade shooter so the player became a Black avatar firing back at symbols of racist power such as police uniforms, court robes, and Klan hoods. They wanted to prove that games could call out bias directly when the narrative centers the people most affected.

Mongrel continued with pieces like Natural Selection, Linker, and Little Big Stories, always teaching communities to remix code, media, and history until they owned the story. BlackLash remains a key example of Afro futurist and critical gaming practice long before the mainstream industry talked about representation.
